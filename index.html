Go Farming - A Community Veggie Sharing App

UI/UX Case Study

Role, Tools & Timeline

  • Role: UI/UX Designer
  • Tools: Figma
  • Timeline: 3 Weeks
  • Type: Self-initiated Project

Problem Statement

Home growers often waste surplus produce. Buyers want fresh, chemical-free vegetables but usually only find bulk market options.

"I had 2 kg of tomatoes last month, and half of it spoiled. I wished there was a way to share or sell it to my neighbors."

Opportunity

Create a hyper-local community app to connect growers and buyers for small-quantity, fresh trades.

Research & Insights

  • Observation: Neighbors already exchange veggies informally, but there is no structured system.
  • Competitor Scan: Grocery apps cater to bulk buying; no focus on home-grown, small quantities.
  • Gap Identified: No dedicated space for community-based, small-quantity trades.

User Interviews

  • Participants: 5 balcony/home growers + 3 frequent veggie buyers.
  • Key Insights: Growers often give away extras informally but wish for a simple selling option. Buyers prefer small, fresh portions instead of bulk and want to see nearby availability first. Trust and simplicity are critical for adoption.

"I usually give away my extra spinach, but I'd love to sell it for a small price if the process was easy."

Personas

Persona 1 - Sumita (The Grower)

  • Age: 42, Housewife, Guwahati
  • Tech comfort: WhatsApp/OLX user
  • Goals: Share/sell surplus produce, connect with neighbors, avoid waste.
  • Frustrations: No platform for small sales, complex posting processes.

Persona 2 - Arnab (The Busy Buyer)

  • Age: 29, Corporate Associate, Bangalore
  • Tech comfort: Regular app user
  • Goals: Quick access to fresh, organic veggies in small amounts.
  • Frustrations: Market veggies lack freshness, doesn't have time to shop around, wants trusted sources.

User Flow & Ideation

Buyer Flow

Browse → Schedule pickup → Select date & time → Confirm → Chat if needed

Seller Flow

Upload → Add details → Publish Listing

Design Process

The design process included creating low-fidelity sketches and wireframes to define the seller and buyer journeys. The focus was on a map view and an explore tab. The design evolved through several iterations.

Final Solution: Key features include a **Map View** to instantly see nearby listings and an **Explore Tab** to browse available veggies easily. The **Chat** feature provides a direct connection with neighbors.

Testing & Feedback

  • Users appreciated the map-first approach since location was their top concern.
  • Growers liked the simple listing flow (upload → publish).
  • Buyers requested clear pickup scheduling, which was added in the final version.

Reflection

User interviews shaped priorities; the map view became the hero feature. A key learning was that simple flows matter more than fancy features, as usability drove adoption.

Next steps

  • Explore adding delivery options, trust-building features (ratings/reviews), and community recipes to increase engagement.

Final Verdict: Go Farming demonstrates how UX can turn an informal community practice into a structured, scalable solution. This project taught me the importance of real user validation and designing for simplicity. Perfect addition to a portfolio to showcase end-to-end UX thinking.
