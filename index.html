01   Why This Matters

The best food in your city is sitting in someone's backyard — and you have no way to find it.

India generates over 70 million tonnes of food waste annually, and a significant share of that happens at home. But the real story isn't just about waste. It's about what gets lost before it even reaches a stranger's table — cold-pressed oils, home-brewed wine, backyard honey, freshly caught river fish, small-batch jams — things you simply cannot buy at a supermarket.

At the same time, urban India's appetite for provenance-driven, chemical-free produce is growing fast. People aren't just looking for fresh food. They're looking for food with a story — grown by someone 2km away, on a balcony they can walk past.

The exchange already happens — on WhatsApp, through word of mouth, over compound walls. Go Farming isn't inventing a behaviour. It's giving an existing behaviour a structure, a trust layer, and a home.

02   The Problem

The informal economy existed. The infrastructure didn't.

I started by talking to people — 5 home growers and 3 buyers in Guwahati and Bengaluru. The same story came up again and again.

"I had an entire batch of homemade wine last season, and I ended up giving most of it away randomly. I had no way to let the right people know it existed."

— Interview participant, home brewer & grower, Guwahati

Growers with surplus avocados, Rohu fish they'd raised themselves, or bottles of fermented produce were either sharing randomly or letting quality go to waste. Buyers were paying premium prices for inferior, mass-market versions of the same things at supermarkets. And the platforms that could connect them — OLX, WhatsApp, even specialty grocery apps — weren't built for this. Wrong scale. No location filter for food. No way to post a listing that says "I have 8 bottles of cold-pressed sesame oil, made last week, 1.5km from you."

The gap wasn't awareness. It wasn't motivation. It was friction, trust, and structure — and those are design problems.

03   How I Fixed It

Design decisions that directly addressed what users needed.

Research gave me a clear picture of what mattered most: proximity, trust, and the ability to negotiate. The design had to serve both a grower posting her first batch of homemade wine and a buyer hunting for freshly pressed avocado oil on his lunch break.

Location as the foundation, not a setting

Proximity isn't a filter. It's the entire value proposition. When someone lists artisanal honey or farm-raised Rohu, the buyer's first question is always "how far?" — not "how cheap?" The home screen leads with your current neighbourhood; every card shows the exact distance. The location picker goes State → City → Neighbourhood, so it works whether you're in Beltola, Guwahati or Koramangala, Bengaluru.

Discovery that reflects how people actually grow

Categories like Homemade Products, Fish, Dairy, Brewery, Flowers, and Mushrooms reflect the real range of what people produce at home — not a supermarket planogram. A buyer browsing for homemade wine should land in Brewery, not scroll past 40 listings of commodities. The "Buy Again" section creates repeat purchase loyalty. "Explore" rewards discovery. Both sides benefit from being shown, not just searched for.

A trust system that's visible, not buried

Nobody pays ₹500/bottle for homemade wine from a stranger without some proof of credibility. Go Farming builds trust in layers: Government ID verification unlocks a visible "Verified" badge on every listing and profile. Star ratings are collected post-transaction. Seller profiles surface past listings, follower count, and a location map. Trust isn't a modal — it's woven into every touchpoint.

A notification system that closes the loop

Every trade generates activity — offer requests, counter-offers, acceptances, rejections. When someone is negotiating on a rare batch of backyard avocados or farm-raised meat, they can't afford to miss a message. The notification centre groups every event by date and makes it searchable, so neither side is left wondering what happened. It turns browsing into active, accountable commerce.

04   Challenges I Faced

Three problems that didn't have obvious answers.

Most of the interesting design work wasn't in building the screens — it was in figuring out the right model before touching Figma. These were the decisions that took the most time.

Challenge 01

How do you enable price negotiation without building a checkout system?

⚠A standard "Add to Cart → Buy Now" model didn't fit. When someone is buying a bottle of homemade wine or a kilogram of forest-foraged mushrooms, the exchange is personal and conversational — the price, quantity, and handoff all get worked out together. A checkout flow would kill that.
✓Negotiation moved into the chat itself. Pre-filled offer chips (₹600, ₹450, ₹400, ₹350, ₹300/kg) give structure to the conversation without forcing a transactional UI. Buyers can tap or type freely. The result feels like texting a neighbour — not completing a purchase.
Challenge 02

How do you make the listing flow simple enough for a first-time seller?

⚠Sumita (42, housewife, Guwahati) grows produce and makes preserves at home. She's a WhatsApp user. If posting a listing felt like filling a government form, she'd close the app before submitting. But a listing for homemade avocado pickle or cold-pressed oil needs to communicate freshness, quantity, availability, and pickup options.
✓The listing flow is split into two focused tabs — Info (what it is, price, when it's ready) and Upload (photos with tips on how to shoot food well). Required fields are starred. The Available From/Till window lets sellers signal freshness — "ready in 3 days, best before Sept" — without needing to update listings daily.

Challenge 03

How do you serve the gift economy and commercial exchange on the same platform?

⚠Several people in research had no interest in selling. They just wanted to give away surplus — a jar of homemade jam, a bunch of herbs, the last few avocados from their tree — without any transaction. Building a separate app wasn't realistic, but mixing free and paid listings without distinction would confuse buyers looking to pay for quality.
✓A single toggle filter — "Free to share" — cleanly separates gift-economy listings from price-based ones. One small UI element holds the entire tension between community generosity and local commerce. Neither feels like a second-class citizen on the platform.
05   Contribution to Society

What this product could do at scale.

Go Farming is a small app solving a very local problem. But the model — hyper-local, trust-layered, conversation-based community commerce — has implications that stretch beyond one city or one harvest.

If 1 in 10 home growers in a single city used this once a week:

  • Quality food stops going to waste — A batch of homemade wine, a kilogram of foraged mushrooms, a tray of avocados — each one reaches someone who genuinely values it, before it's lost. Every listing is a unit of effort and care that doesn't disappear.
  • Premium, chemical-free access becomes local — Urban buyers get cold-pressed oils, farm-raised fish, and small-batch preserves without supermarket mark-ups or cold-chain loss. What was once a farmers' market privilege becomes a neighbourhood habit.
  • Micro income with real dignity — A grower earning ₹500–₹2000/week from homemade wine or specialty produce isn't a business. But it's real income, with no commute, no middleman, and no platform taking a cut.
  • Neighbourhood trust, built trade by trade — Every completed transaction is a human connection. A verified profile. A star given. A follow made. The app builds social capital as a by-product of commerce.
  • A model that travels — What works for homemade wine in Guwahati works for cold-pressed oils in Bengaluru, foraged mushrooms in Pune, or farm-raised poultry in Mumbai. The infrastructure is the same; the community fills it with whatever it grows best.
06   Reflection

What I learned, and what I'd do differently.

What worked

Starting from an existing informal behaviour — WhatsApp food swaps between neighbours — meant the product had a real user base before a single screen was designed. The research didn't generate the insight; it confirmed something that was already happening and helped me design with it, not against it.

Putting negotiation inside chat rather than building a checkout was the right call. It made the product feel like a community tool — not a marketplace. That distinction matters enormously when trust is the first barrier to adoption. Someone willing to buy a stranger's homemade Rohu pickle needs to feel like they're messaging a person, not processing a transaction.

What I'd do differently

I assumed offer chip denominations (₹600, ₹450, ₹400...) based on interview data from Guwahati. But price expectations differ sharply by product type and city — negotiating for a bottle of cold-pressed avocado oil in Bengaluru is a very different conversation from haggling over fresh catch in Guwahati. I'd run usability testing on the offer flow across product categories before shipping.

I'd also test the listing flow with non-tech-savvy users far earlier. Watching someone like Sumita navigate the Info → Upload tab split live would surface friction I can't anticipate from research notes alone.

What's next

Three features that would make this product genuinely indispensable: a Growth Tracker (a plant diary that predicts harvest windows and auto-drafts listings before produce is even ready), Community Recipes (connect surplus produce to what to cook — a buyer who sees "homemade wine + avocados nearby" should see what to make with both), and Delivery Coordination (a lightweight map-based pickup scheduler that removes the last friction from every trade).
